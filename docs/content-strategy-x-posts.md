#### 选题 1.1：被品牌拖欠款的真实数据

**X 形式**：Thread（数据驱动型）
**目标**：r/UGCcreators 群体的核心愤怒点

```
87% of creators have been paid late by brands.
33% have NEVER been paid at all.

I tracked 100 creator-brand deals across 6 platforms. Here's what nobody talks about:

🧵

1/ The payment problem is worse than you think.

Average payment delay: Net 120 (that's 4 months after content is live)
72% of freelancers have been ghosted by a client at least once
$1.3B-$4.6B lost to influencer fraud annually

This isn't a "bad apple" problem. It's a SYSTEM problem.

2/ The platforms don't protect you.

The big platforms (Aspire, GRIN, Upfluence) charge brands $2K-$35K/year.
They'll help brands FIND you. But they won't guarantee you get PAID.

Why? Because the brand is their customer. Not you.

3/ There's a simple fix that almost no one uses: escrow.

Brand puts the money in BEFORE you start.
You put down a pledge (returned when you deliver).
Both sides have skin in the game.

Ghosting becomes expensive. Bad actors self-filter out.

4/ This isn't theoretical.

Escrow is standard in:
- Real estate (earnest money)
- Freelance platforms (Upwork escrow)
- Crypto (smart contracts)

But somehow, the creator economy skipped this layer entirely.

5/ What to look for in a brand deal:

✅ Money locked upfront (not "we'll pay after")
✅ Clear deliverables defined before you start
✅ Timeline with consequences for BOTH sides
✅ Third party holding the funds

If any of these are missing → you're taking 100% of the risk.

6/ The power dynamic needs to flip.

Right now: creator does work → hopes brand pays
How it should be: brand locks payment → creator delivers → funds auto-release

This isn't radical. It's how every other professional service works.

You wouldn't build someone's house without a contract and deposit.
Why would you build their brand without the same?

If you're a creator who's been burned, drop your story below.
I'm collecting data on how bad this really is.
```

#### 选题 1.3：如何识别一个靠谱的品牌合作（实操清单）

**X 形式**：Thread（清单型）
**目标**：实用价值 → 收藏/转发

```
I've done 50+ brand deals as a small creator. Some great, some terrible.

Here's my 5-point checklist to spot a good brand before you say yes:

🧵

1/ Do they talk about payment upfront?

🚩 Red flag: "We'll discuss compensation later" or "exposure-based"
✅ Green flag: They name a number in the first message

Legit brands know creators need to get paid. They lead with the offer.

2/ Do they have a clear brief?

🚩 Red flag: "Just make something cool" (you'll revise 10 times)
✅ Green flag: Deliverables, format, timeline, usage rights — all written down

Vague brief = unlimited revisions = you lose money.

3/ Check their history with other creators.

🚩 Red flag: No tagged creators, or creators who won't talk about them
✅ Green flag: Consistent UGC from the same creators over time

DM one of their past creators. Ask: "Did they pay on time?"

4/ How do they handle revisions?

🚩 Red flag: "We'll know it when we see it"
✅ Green flag: "2 rounds of revisions included, $X per additional round"

Your time is the product. Treat it that way.

5/ Where's the money right now?

This is the big one. If the brand can't prove they've set aside your payment, you're lending them free labor.

Platforms with escrow solve this. Without it, you're trusting a stranger on the internet.

Bottom line: The best predictor of a good deal is whether the brand has real consequences if they flake.

No consequences = no guarantee.
```

**知识点来源**：[[供应商评估框架]]（五维评估逻辑迁移到品牌评估）、pledge economy设计原理

#### 选题 2.1：Amazon卖家做KOC营销的数学题

**X 形式**：Thread（数据+算账）
**目标**：Amazon/跨境电商卖家

```
I spent $2,000 on an influencer who sent me 1 video with 47 views.
Then I did the math and realized the problem wasn't the influencer.

Here's what I learned about KOC marketing for Amazon sellers:

🧵

1/ The unit economics don't work if you go big.

Mega influencer: $2K-$10K for one post
Typical conversion rate: 0.5-2%
You need 1,000-5,000 clicks to break even on a $30 product

Meanwhile: 5 micro-KOCs × $50 in product + $50 commission = $500 total.
Each one makes authentic content. Combined reach is often HIGHER.

2/ The real problem isn't finding creators. It's trust.

Scenario A: You send free product. Creator ghosts. You lose product + shipping.
Scenario B: You pay upfront. Creator sends low-effort content. You can't get money back.
Scenario C: You pay after. Creator demands payment. Content underperformed. Dispute.

Every path has risk. The platform should absorb this risk.

3/ What an escrow system changes:

- You pre-pay the commission pool (locked, visible to creator)
- Creator puts down their own pledge (skin in the game)
- Content delivered → both sides get their money back + creator gets paid
- Creator flakes → your commission returns to you, creator forfeits pledge

Now both sides lose if the deal fails. That changes behavior.

4/ The math for a $30 product with 10 KOCs:

Traditional: $500 in product + shipping. Hope 5/10 post. Maybe 2 are good. ROI unclear.
With escrow: Same $500. 10 creators with skin in the game. Higher completion rate. Performance tracked.

The platform fee ($5/task) is cheaper than one ghosted shipment.

5/ Why this matters now specifically:

- iOS changes killed FB ad ROAS → need organic content
- Amazon Attribution lets you track off-platform traffic
- TikTok Shop is training creators to expect brand deals
- AI search (ChatGPT, Perplexity) surfaces creator content as answers

KOC content isn't just marketing. It's becoming your SEO moat.

Bottom line: The smartest Amazon sellers I know are building KOC armies. Not influencer one-offs. The difference is systems + trust infrastructure.
```

```
As an e-commerce seller, you already know how to vet a supplier:

1. Check samples before bulk order
2. Verify credentials (business license, certifications)
3. Start small, scale after trust is built
4. Have a contract with clear consequences

Why aren't you doing the same with KOCs?

🧵

1/ The parallel is exact.

Supplier evaluation = KOC evaluation
Sample order = Test campaign
Production capacity = Content consistency
Compliance cert = Past brand deal history

You'd never wire $5000 to a factory you found yesterday.
Why send $500 in product + commission with zero verification?

2/ Your supplier framework, adapted for KOCs:

Q1: Can they deliver? → Content portfolio review (not just follower count)
Q2: Are they compliant? → Past brand feedback / platform trust score
Q3: What's their capacity? → How many active deals can they handle?
Q4: What are the terms? → Clear deliverables, timeline, revision policy
Q5: What happens if they fail? → Escrow consequences for non-delivery

Same 5 dimensions. Different counterparty.

3/ The escrow layer fixes what contracts can't.

Contract with a Chinese factory → enforceable (trade law exists)
Contract with a creator in another country → good luck enforcing that

Escrow doesn't need enforcement. The money is already locked.
Creator delivers → auto-released.
Creator doesn't → auto-returned.

This is the only model that works cross-border without a legal team.

4/ The irony:

Cross-border sellers deal with supplier risk, platform risk, logistics risk, compliance risk, currency risk, AND marketing risk — but somehow marketing risk is the one they just... hope works out.

Apply the same rigor to KOCs that you do to suppliers.
Or use a platform that bakes it in.
```
